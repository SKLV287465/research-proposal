Microkernels are theoretically and aesthetically better than monolithic kernels. Microkernels inherently promote a more modular and perhaps more portable design. More importantly, microkernels allow for a smaller \gls{tcb} which can be reduced into smaller modular components which decreases the probability and frequency of bugs and also makes it feasible to formally verify parts of the \gls{tcb} to create a more trustworthy system. However, the general perception of microkernels is that while \gls{mkbs}'s are theoretically good, they fall short in real life due to lacking performance. However, this perception is not strongly backed, and this thesis aims to prove that this is not true and a well designed gls{mkbs} based on a well optimised microkernel such as seL4 whether dynamic or static should not be bottlenecked by the microkernel or the inherent design of a \gls{mkbs}.
